After the Great Flood:

An Interactive Research Project about Nashville's Great Flood of 2010

Gallery

Background

The Great Flood of 2010 in Nashville resulted in approximately $2 billion dollars in damage. Previous literature have noted that natural disasters create long term effects on local areas and residents. Natural disasters have been shown to affect socioeconomic factors associated with neighborhoods and residents. The socioeconomic gap was found to widen in the years following natural disasters (Lee 2018). Recovery and coping from Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans, Louisana was found by Masozera, Bailey and Kerchner (2007) to be impacted by socioeconomic status as well. Cutter, Ash and Emrich advocated for taking a different approach to disaster resilience, since their research found that urban and rural counties each have differing factors that contribute to how they recover from disasters.

Geographic research on socioeconomics and neighborhood effects have examined their roles in topics like health (Moody et al. 2016; Koh et al. 2015) and residential segregation (Darden and Rubalcava 2018; Darden 2010; Darden 2000). Cutter, Ash and Emrich (2016) found, in their geographic research, that socioeconomics did play a role in disaster recovery, Cutter et al. used a county level analysis to examine recovery across the United States. In relation to residential segregation, researchers have found that several racial groups live disproportionately in neighborhoods with lower neighborhood socioeconomic characteristics. In metropolitan areas, there is spatial stratification of income across neighborhoods (Darden 2010; Darden 2000)

For instance, in Metropolitan Detroit, Michigan, suburban communities have been shown to comprise residents with higher neighborhood socioeconomic characteristics than in the central city of the metropolitan areas (Darden 2010; Darden 2000). This project asked first: What was the socioeconomic condition of neighborhoods after the flood in Nashville? The project looked at the same socioeconomic variables before the flood as well as after the flood to understand if any neighborhood changes occurred. Most literature have researched natural disasters like Hurricane Katrina to understand neighborhood change, but currently, there is no study that has looked at the neighborhood effects of the Great Flood of 2010 on Nashville residents.


Significance

The project offers new insight into how Nashville neighborhoods have changed since the Great Flood of 2010. This project, to my knowledge, is the first that has conducted the Modified Darden-Kamel Composite Socioeconomic Index to 1. Determine the spatial and socioeconomic stratification of neighborhood effects in Metropolitan Nashville Tennessee and 2. To determine the neighborhood effects related to a natural disaster. This information will be useful in understanding how specific areas and populations experience economic recovery after natural disasters but also sheds light on the spatial inequalities that were and continue to exist in the Metropolitan Nashville Area.

Even before the flood, Non-Hispanic Whites, Non-Hispanic Blacks and Hispanics were living disproportionally in urban and suburban neighborhoods throughout the metropolitan area. Flood or no flood, this is an issue. Further analysis is needed to determine if inequalities existed in federal aid distribution and/or if inequalities continue into the next five- year period 2016-2020 in terms of race and socioeconomics. This will be helpful to identifying any issues that will need action before the next natural disaster. As noted, natural disasters affect everyone in its path, but there have been inequalities shown in who recovers the most for these natural disasters.
