After the Great Flood:

An Interactive Research Project about Nashville's Great Flood of 2010

Data and Methodology

This project used data from two sources: 1) from the Nashville GIS Department and 2) from the U.S. Census Bureau’s American Community Survey 5 year estimates from 2005-2009 and 2011-2015.

American Community Survey, 5-Year Estimates, 2005-2009 and 2011-2015

The U.S. Census Bureau offers annual (1), triennial (3), and quinquennial (5) and decennial (10) year data estimates on American households. The American Community Survey (ACS) collects data on social, economic, housing and demographic characteristics. The ACS presents data on characteristics such as education, marital status, occupation, health insurance coverage, housing occupancy, rent, age and race at various geographical levels. This project used the five-year estimates for 2005-2009 and 2011-2015 to represent the socieconomic and racial compositions of neighborhoods five years before and after the flood.

Nashville GIS Department: Flood Data

The Nashville GIS Department collected data on the Great Flood of 2010, which provided census tract level information such as damage levels, building age and property type. The original dataset contained a total of 11,647 properties and 150 unique census tracts. This project only used census tracts that were located in Metropolitan Nashville and had socioeconomic positions (SEPs) in 2009 and 2015. Census tracts outside of the Metropolitan Nashville Area and without SEPs for 2009 and 2015 were excluded from this study. Only 97 unique census tracts were analyzed for this project.


Methodology

Using the Modified Darden-Kamel Composite Socioeconomic Index, this project identified SEPs for census tracts in the Metropolitan Nashville Area.

The Modified Darden-Kamel Composite Socioeconomic Index

The Modified Darden-Kamel Composite Socioeconomic Index (D-K CSI) uses quantitative methods to identify socioeconomic positions (SEP) for census tracts within a two- or three-county area. Using the U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey, the methodology determines spatial inequality by evaluating the following nine socioeconomic characteristics:

  1. poverty
  2. unemployment rate
  3. median household income
  4. percentage of vehicle ownership
  5. percentage of professional & managerial workers
  6. percentage of people holding a bachelor’s degree or other higher education
  7. median house value
  8. median monthly rent
  9. percentage of home ownership

This methodology has been previously used in health-related studies such as Moody et al. (2016), Darden & Rubalcava (2018), Barnes (2018) and Wilcox (2020). The articles focus on Metropolitan Detroit and research topics including blood lead levels, residential segregation among Hispanic and Non-Hispanic White population, asthma hospitalizations and health outcomes of diabetes, obesity and lack of sleep. Darden et al. (2010) first used the methodology to research residential segregation among Black and White populations. The Modified D-K CSI has not evaluated the socioeconomic effects of natural disasters.

Limitations

Limitation 1: The damage level was determined by each property owner and may be subjective. The collected data on the flood of 2010 does not include information on the damage level by race of property owner, which would be helpful to determine patterns in perceived damage level by race/ethnicity. The American Community Survey was instead employed to provide information on the racial composition of each census tract and calculated to find the average compositions for each SEP. The racial compositions of each SEP were found and used to determine which racial groups were overpopulated in each SEP in relation to socioeocnomic status and damage level. Although the owner's racial/ethnic information was not included in the dataset, the American Community Survey offered information for each of the census tracts analyzed in this study. This project was able to identify if one or more proportions of a race were uneven in different socioeconomic positions

Limitation 2: Some census tracts from 2005-2009 could not be analyzed because they had no SEP. To make the study completely comparable, data was excluded if it did not exist in 2005-2009 and 2011-2015 datasets. The American Community Survey data for 2005-2009 contained several census tracts in the Metropolitan Nashville Area that had missing variable data related the nine variables used in this analysis. Although excluding the data removed 53 census tracts from being analyzed, the project still maintained and analyzed approximately 64% of the census tracts in the original datasets.
